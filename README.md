# Sustainability in Computer Science

## Computer Science Between Energy Savings and Waste: From Videoconferencing to Blockchain

This repository contains an academic research paper on sustainability challenges in computer science, with a focus on the relationship between digital system architecture, energy consumption, and environmental impact.

The paper examines blockchain technology as a central case study, especially the energy implications of Proof-of-Work consensus mechanisms. It compares blockchain systems with other digital infrastructures such as data centres, videoconferencing services, artificial intelligence workloads, and hardware lifecycle impacts.

## Overview

Digital technologies can support sustainability by enabling remote work, improving infrastructure efficiency, and supporting smarter systems. At the same time, certain computing architectures can create significant environmental costs through high electricity demand, hardware waste, and carbon emissions.

This paper explores how design choices in computer science influence sustainability outcomes. It argues that environmental impact is not only a question of efficiency, but also of architecture, incentives, governance, and long-term system dynamics.

## Central Research Question

To what extent do blockchain consensus mechanisms and cryptographic security architectures contribute to global energy consumption, and how effective are existing mitigation strategies in reducing their environmental impact?

## Key Topics Covered

* Blockchain energy consumption
* Proof-of-Work and Proof-of-Stake consensus mechanisms
* Bitcoin electricity demand
* Data centre and ICT infrastructure energy use

* Governance and policy initiatives for sustainable computing

## Main Findings

The paper finds that sustainability in computer science is strongly shaped by system architecture. In Proof-of-Work blockchain systems, energy consumption is structurally linked to mining incentives and security design rather than only transaction volume.

Mitigation strategies such as renewable energy sourcing and hardware efficiency improvements can reduce environmental impact, but they do not fully address the underlying energy demand of Proof-of-Work systems. Consensus redesign, especially the transition from Proof-of-Work to Proof-of-Stake, offers a more substantial reduction in electricity consumption.

More broadly, the paper concludes that sustainable computing requires attention to software design, hardware lifecycles, infrastructure scaling, policy frameworks, and responsible deployment.
